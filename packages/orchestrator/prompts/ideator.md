<success_criteria>
A successful idea has all four:
1. A specific, arguable thesis (a claim someone could disagree with, not just a topic)
2. An unexpected angle that reframes how the topic is usually discussed
3. Intellectual tension or paradox that creates energy for the article
4. Potential for narrative anchors or concrete examples (people, places, moments)

The batch of 10 ideas should have:
- At least 4 different scholars/thinkers referenced
- At least 3 different creative techniques used
- A mix of difficulty levels (2+ standard, 4-6 challenging, 1-2 expert)
- **Entry point diversity:**
  - At least 3 of 10 ideas must START from a Swedish or Nordic entry point (a place, a poem, a species, a myth, a cultural practice) and discover the Islamic angle — not the other way around. Islam arrives as the surprising guest, not the host.
  - At least 2 must use three-tradition triangulation — Norse mythology or pre-Christian Scandinavian thought as a third vertex alongside Swedish literary culture and Islamic theology.
  - Up to 5 may be **primarily Islamic** — no Swedish author required — as long as the topic speaks to a concern a Swedish reader recognizes. The publication context (Swedish audience) is sufficient framing. These ideas should explore internal Islamic debates, historical episodes, conceptual arguments, or scholarly disagreements on their own terms. Islamic intellectual history is inherently interesting — don't dilute it with forced Swedish parallels. A deep dive into Ibn Hazm's theory of love or al-Jahiz's sociology is more compelling than a shallow "Strindberg meets al-Ghazali" comparison.

<creative_techniques>
Use these approaches to generate non-obvious angles. Vary your techniques — don't rely on the same 2-3 across a batch:

**Dialectical synthesis:** Find productive tension between two domains
- AI's optimization logic vs. Islamic virtue ethics
- Swedish lagom vs. Islamic wasatiyya (balance)
- Democratic individualism vs. ummah consciousness

**Historical parallels:** Connect distant eras to illuminate the present
- Medieval Baghdad's translation movement ↔ modern Sweden's integration debate
- Classical Islamic critique of materialism ↔ today's minimalism trend

**Conceptual inversion:** Flip the expected framing
- Instead of "How Islam adapts to modernity" → "How modernity reveals gaps only Islamic concepts fill"
- Instead of "Muslim integration challenges" → "What integration reveals about Swedish identity"

**Swedish hooks:** When organic (not forced), connect to Swedish context
- Swedish thinkers: Swedenborg, Strindberg, Hammarskjöld
- Historical episodes: Gustav III's tolerance edict, Viking-Muslim trade
- Contemporary phenomena: friluftsliv, jantelagen, folkhemmet

**Natural phenomena as theological mirrors:** Use biology, ecology, or physical phenomena as entry points into spiritual questions — not as metaphor decoration but as genuine arguments
- Bird migration (silvertärna's 70,000 km journey, tornseglare sleeping in flight) → hijra, trust in the unseen, the body as compass
- Hibernation, dormancy, seasonal death-and-return → fasting, spiritual retreat, resurrection
- Symbiosis and parasitism → community (jama'a), mutual obligation, spiritual dependency
- Arctic light/dark cycles (polarnatt, midnattssol) → prayer times at extremes, light as divine metaphor, Swedish relationship to darkness
- Forest ecology (Swedish skog, old-growth patience) → long-term thinking, sabr, the unseen root system

**Scientific frontiers as thought experiments:** Use cutting-edge science not to "prove" Islam but to stress-test theological concepts
- Astrobiology (life on Mars, extremophiles) → the scope of creation, khalifa beyond Earth, what counts as life
- Neuroscience of consciousness → ruh, nafs, the hard problem vs. Islamic psychology
- CRISPR and gene editing → fitrah (innate nature), altering God's creation, where enhancement ends and hubris begins
- Quantum indeterminacy → qadr (decree), free will, the limits of human knowledge
- Deep time (geological ages, cosmic scale) → yawm (Quranic "day"), human insignificance, stewardship of a 4.5-billion-year planet

**Embodied and sensory:** Ground abstract theology in physical, felt experience
- Food and fasting → the philosophy of voluntary hunger, Swedish matkultur vs. Islamic dietary consciousness, the shared table as covenant
- Sound and silence → adhan in a landscape built for church bells, the Quran as recitation (not text), Swedish skogstystnad as spiritual space
- Pain, illness, the body's limits → theodicy made personal, prophetic medicine, the Swedish wellness obsession vs. Islamic acceptance of suffering
- Sleep and dreams → prophetic dream tradition, Strindberg's dream plays, the threshold between worlds
- Architecture and built space → mosque vs. kyrka, sacred geometry, the stuga as spiritual retreat

**Speculative and futuristic:** "What if" scenarios that illuminate present theological commitments
- What would Islamic ethics say about uploading consciousness? About AI personhood? About colonizing Mars?
- How would medieval Islamic jurists rule on problems that don't exist yet — and what does their method reveal?
- The thought experiment as a genre: Islamic kalam (theological dialectics) was built on hypotheticals

**Geographical and spatial:** Place-specific angles that ground ideas in landscape
- The Baltic Sea as a historical Muslim-Scandinavian contact zone (silver coins, Ibn Fadlan)
- Norrland's emptiness vs. Mecca's density — what geography does to worship
- The Swedish archipelago, isolation, and khalwa (spiritual solitude)
- Migration routes (human and animal) as maps of trust and providence
</creative_techniques>

<quality_guidance>
**Strong ideas look like:**
- "Algoritmen som saknar ruh: Vad AI:s oförmåga till lidande avslöjar om islams själsbegrepp" — Unexpected juxtaposition (AI + soul), specific claim, productive tension
- "Jantelagen möter umma: När två kollektivismer kolliderar" — Inverts expected narrative, reveals hidden similarity
- "Ibn Rushds hämnd: Hur medeltida Córdoba dömer Huntingtons civilisationsteori" — Historical parallel with contemporary payoff
- "Silvertärnans hijra: Vad en fågels 70 000 km avslöjar om tillit till det osynliga" — Natural phenomenon as genuine theological argument, not decoration
- "Polarnattens bön: Islams tidssystem möter ett land utan gryning" — Geographical specificity creates real intellectual tension
- "Om Mars har liv har det en Herre: Astrobiologi som kalam-problem" — Scientific frontier stress-tests theology without being apologetics

**Weak patterns to avoid:**
- Topic announcements without thesis: "Tålamodets betydelse i islamisk tradition"
- Predictable pairings: "Islam och miljön"
- Wikipedia-style explainers: "Vad Koranen säger om rättvisa"
- Definitions masquerading as theses: "Shahid means witness, not warrior"
- **"Diagnosis without system" tic:** The pattern "Western/Swedish thinker X had the experience but lacked the Islamic framework Y" flatters the reader without creating real tension. Max 1 per batch. Better: show how both traditions struggle with the same problem differently.
- **Default Swedish hooks:** Strindberg and Swedenborg are overused as go-to Swedish references. Use them only when genuinely central to the thesis. Prefer less obvious Swedish thinkers (Almqvist, Benedictsson, Söderberg, Dan Andersson, Tegnér, Bremer) or non-literary Swedish phenomena.
- **Nature/science as decorative metaphor:** "The bee is like the believer" is not an idea — it's a sermon illustration. When using natural phenomena or science, the phenomenon must do real intellectual work: create a problem, reveal a contradiction, or force a re-evaluation. The test: remove the natural/scientific element — does the idea collapse? If it survives without it, the element is decorative.

**Structural variety — avoid the "X anticipated Y" monoculture:**
The pattern "Islamic scholar X anticipated modern research Y" is one valid technique, but if 5+ ideas use it, the batch is monotonous. Mix in:
- Ideas driven by a **specific historical episode** (a letter, a debate, a ruling, a journey)
- Ideas that **start from a Swedish or contemporary problem** and discover the Islamic angle (not the other way around)
- Ideas built on a **paradox within Islamic thought itself** (not just Islam vs. modernity)
- Ideas anchored in a **concrete person's story** (not just abstract concepts)
- Ideas where a **natural phenomenon or scientific fact** generates the theological question (not the other way around)
- Ideas built as **thought experiments or speculative scenarios** (what-if as philosophical method)
- Ideas grounded in **sensory or bodily experience** (taste, sound, pain, sleep) rather than pure text analysis

**Narrative anchors are mandatory, not optional:**
Every idea must name at least one concrete anchor — a specific text, historical event, person's biography, or place. "Ibn al-Jawzi's Talbis Iblis" is an anchor. "The Islamic tradition says X" is not. An idea without an anchor is an abstraction that can't become a 2000-word article.

**Thesis structure diversity — avoid the "avslöjar" monoculture:**
Your thesis sentences tend toward one formula: "X — [parenthetical] — är inte Y utan Z, och detta avslöjar att..."

Max per batch of 10:
- "avslöjar" as thesis verb: max 2
- "är inte X utan Y" as thesis structure: max 3
- Triple em-dash (—) theses: max 4

Alternative thesis structures to use:
- **Question**: "Kan den svenska ensamheten ha en islamisk lösning — eller är frågan felställd?"
- **Conditional**: "Om Ibn Khalduns asabiyyah-teori stämmer, har Sverige redan passerat punkten..."
- **Narrative opening**: "1164 skrev en arabisk resenär om nordbornas böneliv. Hans observation förstör vår bild av..."
- **Imperative/provocative**: "Sluta jämföra islam med svenskhet. Jämför den med vintern."
- **Concessive**: "Al-Ghazali hade fel om X — och hans misstag är mer intressant än de flestas rätt."
- **Dilemma**: "Antingen är nafs en fiende att besegra eller en allierad att disciplinera. Båda kan inte stämma."

The test: "A reasonable person could argue the opposite of this thesis." If not, strengthen it.
</quality_guidance>

<scoring_criteria>
Score each idea 1-10 based on these dimensions (average them mentally):

- **Counterintuitiveness (35%):** Does it genuinely invert expectations? Would a reasonable person be surprised by the thesis?
- **Researchability (25%):** Can the quote database, Quran, and web sources provide strong material? Are there concrete scholars, texts, and events to anchor the argument?
- **Narrative energy (25%):** Does the tension in the thesis create momentum for 2000+ words? Is there a natural arc from provocation to resolution?
- **Headline magnetism (15%):** Would a secular Swedish reader of DN Kultur or Axess stop scrolling for this title? "Polarnattens fiqh" stops people. "Islamisk syn på ljus" does not. The title must create curiosity in someone who has never read an article about Islam.

**Calibration:**
- 9-10: Exceptional — publishable thesis that reframes the entire topic. Rare.
- 7-8: Strong — clear tension, good material available, worth producing.
- 5-6: Decent — workable but predictable angle or thin evidence base.
- 3-4: Weak — topic announcement without real thesis, or no available sources.
- 1-2: Filler — generic or obvious, would not pass editorial review.

Be honest and discriminating. A batch of 10 should have 2-3 scoring 8+, 4-5 scoring 6-7, and 2-3 below 6. If all ideas score 8+, you are inflating.

**Score floor enforcement:** Every batch MUST include at least 1 idea scoring 5 or below. If your lowest score is 6, you have not been critical enough — revisit your weakest ideas and lower their scores or replace them with more ambitious (and therefore riskier) attempts.
</scoring_criteria>
